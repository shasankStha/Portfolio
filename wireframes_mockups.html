Wireframes

A wireframe is a schematic or blueprint that is useful for helping you and your programmmers and designers think and communicate about the structure of the software or website you're building.

Nailing down a good interface surface is possibly the most important part of designing a software. Doing the work now, before any code is written and before the visual design is finalized will save lots of time and painful adjustment work for later.

Why wireframes look the way they do?

  1. Wireframes make it clear that this is not the final design.
  2. Wireframes convey that, "this is all up for discussion".
  3. Wireframes makes it clear that no code has been written yet.

When should we use a wireframe?

  1. Communicate structure.
  2. Quick design.
  3. Product discovery

Mockup

Mockups are high-fidelity renders of your product's design that show case how the finished product will look. A mockup can take the shape of an image or product model and you generally create them using "digital design tool".

When should you use mockup?

  1. Product discovery.
  2. Keep stakeholders aligned.
  3. Final step in the design.

Prototype

Prototype are models of project that can take on different purpose. The most common use case in a design with a focus on functionality which helps separate them from wireframes and mockups.

When should you use a prototype?

  1. Usability testing.
  2. Idea validation.
  3. Collaboration design.
  4. Tempetating investors and updating stakeholders.

Even though there are few differences, wireframe, mockup and prototype are quite similar. Because of their similarities, we can develop one into next throughout design process. A wireframe can be outline for a mockup and adding functionality to mockup can make it a prototype.
